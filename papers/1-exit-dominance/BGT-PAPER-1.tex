% ============================================================
% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
\textbf{Abstract.} In a multipolar world with no trusted monetary
coordinator, how do rational capital allocators preserve value when
sovereign settlement systems are subject to debasement and political
capture? The question is not hypothetical: the 2022 freezing of
Russian foreign-exchange reserves demonstrated that sovereign settlement
is capturable for any holder, central banks included. This paper models the choice as
a non-cooperative game (the ``Exit Game'') in which allocators choose
between capturable settlement systems (``Stay'') and neutral settlement
(``Exit''). The model rests on four assumptions: persistent
multipolarity, rational self-interest, computational hardness, and the
persistence of network effects.
Three results follow. First, the payoff advantage of Exit over
Stay grows as adoption rises (Proposition~1). Second, no Stay
coalition is internally stable once any allocator's threshold is
reached (Proposition~2). Third, above a critical adoption threshold Stay
coalitions cannot reform, and persistent debasement drives that threshold
downward over time (Theorem~1). Together these
establish Bitcoin as the primary candidate for neutral settlement
derived purely from ongoing incentives (Theorem~2). The formal Exit Game results show
viable neutral settlement creates a new form of competition for the
existing monetary order.
\end{abstract}

\section{1. Introduction}

A capital allocator with purchasing power to protect faces a choice between two settlement systems. The first is the prevailing one: sovereign currency, custodied by regulated intermediaries, settled through banking and securities infrastructure. Call this Stay. The second is a settlement asset that no government issues, no intermediary custodies, and no political body can debase or freeze. Call this Exit. This is the Exit Game. The question is not which system any one allocator prefers. It is whether the repeated incentives will push a critical mass of capital from Stay to Exit.

While partial exit via gold has existed for thousands of years, true exit only became possible when Nakamoto\textsuperscript{21} mined his first block on 3 January 2009. In the genesis block of Bitcoin he encoded that morning's front-page headline from \emph{The Times} of London: ``Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks.'' Since 2009, hashrate on the Bitcoin network has only ever trended higher, despite sovereign mining bans and several hostile hard-fork events. Adoption by ever larger cohorts of capital (ETFs, pension funds, sovereigns) suggests rational allocators continue to defect to Exit.

The usual measure of debasement risk is debt-to-GDP. Public debt is near 100\% of GDP and projected at 115\% by the early 2030s (IMF\textsuperscript{16}). At that scale, history says the debt gets paid down by debasing the currency, not by taxes (Reinhart and Sbrancia\textsuperscript{23}). High debt is not itself new: what is new is what governments are now committed to spend through the 2030s with no tax base behind it. The United States is stepping back from its role as world military referee, and NATO members pledged in June 2025 to spend 5\% of GDP on defense by 2035. The logic in every arms race is the same: spend more or get left behind (Richardson\textsuperscript{24}; Powell\textsuperscript{22}). AI is the same race: better AI means better weapons, so winning compute means winning force. AI capex in 2026 runs at a multiple of Apollo's peak GDP share (\(\sim\)0.7\% in 1966), and China is investing at comparable scale through state direction. Neither side can stop without losing the lead (Armstrong, Bostrom, and Shulman\textsuperscript{1}). The Exit Game does not require any specific catalyst to work. The emerging AI-military arms race trends above set the backdrop because they are the largest known debasement catalysts today.

The deeper point in the genesis block was the subtle nod to the Cantillon effect: in every fiat system, those nearest to issuance capture the wealth transfer that debasement creates (Cantillon\textsuperscript{8}). Will aggregate capital flee to a new system where no central issuer can benefit from the Cantillon effect to debase the many to enrich the few? This paper turns that question into a formal game.

\section{2. Related Work}

The prevailing policy treatment of this coordination problem is
Brunnermeier, James, and Landau\textsuperscript{6}, who describe the
problem verbally without a formal game-theoretic derivation. They prescribe
central bank digital currency as the institutional response. This
paper agrees with the coordination problem but disagrees with the
proposed CBDC solution: a CBDC remains sovereign-issued, freezable,
and debaseable, fails Definition~1, and is therefore an instance of
Stay that Theorem~2 covers.

The existing Bitcoin game-theory literature analyzes the
mining subgame that upholds this paper's
computational-hardness assumption (Assumption~3). Biais,
Bisiere, Bouvard, and Casamatta\textsuperscript{5} show that following the
longest chain is an equilibrium of the miner game. Eyal and
Sirer\textsuperscript{10} show that miners can profit by secretly withholding
newly found blocks instead of publishing them. Budish\textsuperscript{7} links the security
budget to attackable stock value: recurring miner rewards must
remain large relative to the one-off benefit of a majority
attack. Chen\textsuperscript{9} closes the loop with a general-equilibrium
model in which Bitcoin's price and ledger security are jointly
determined. Together these results establish that the protocol
produces blocks and resists attack at prices endogenous to
adoption. This paper inherits them as Assumption~3 and bridges
the gap above the mining subgame to the macro coordination problem.

\section{3. Model}

\subsection{3.1 Maintained Assumptions}

The analysis rests on four assumptions. All subsequent claims derive from these assumptions; rejecting any assumption invalidates the specific claims that depend on it.

\begin{assumption}[Multipolarity]
Power is split across power centers where no center can exert unilateral control of the settlement layer.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Rational Self-Interest]
Actors optimize for self-interest. When defection from cooperative
agreements is unpunished or unpunishable, actors defect.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Computational Hardness]
Certain mathematical problems cannot be solved in practical time by any known algorithm. Digital scarcity and cryptographic custody are implementable.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Network Effect Persistence]
Past critical mass, switching costs exceed marginal gains of alternatives. An established network attracts further adopters; its lead compounds (Katz and Shapiro\textsuperscript{17}).
\end{assumption}

\subsection{3.2 Definitions}

\begin{definition}[Neutral Settlement]
An asset $S$ is a \emph{neutral settlement asset} if and only if $S$ is immune to capture by force, debasement, or coordinated political action. Property P6 (neutrality) corresponds to what Huberman, Leshno, and Moallemi\textsuperscript{15} term ``monopoly without a monopolist'': the protocol exhibits monopoly-like network effects without an entity capable of exercising monopoly power. The cryptographic and consensus primitives that ground P1--P3 and P5--P6 trace to Nakamoto\textsuperscript{21}. P1--P7 are assumed throughout. So is the miner-subgame result of \S2.
\end{definition}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption*{\textbf{Table 1.} The seven properties jointly necessary for Definition~1. Each row names what an asset must guarantee and the attack class it forecloses.}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Property & Blocks \\
\midrule
P1: Informational security & Physical confiscation \\
P2: Protocol security & Protocol rule change \\
P3: Permissionless access & Transaction censorship \\
P4: Absolute scarcity & Supply inflation \\
P5: Cheap finality & Prohibitive cost/delay \\
P6: Neutrality & Governance takeover \\
P7: Adaptive resilience & Obsolescence \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent\emph{Gold as Neutral Settlement.} Gold has been synonymous with neutral settlement across five millennia. Density, divisibility, recognizability, and aesthetic appeal sustained it as money in the market-selection account of monetary emergence (Menger\textsuperscript{19}). Wampum, cowrie shells, and salt shared the surface properties but failed P4 once industrial production, oceanic shipping, and refined extraction broke their scarcity. Only gold has held P4 across that span. Arslanalp, Eichengreen, and Simpson-Bell\textsuperscript{2} trace the post-2022 central-bank gold buying to financial-sanctions risk on the main reserve issuers, not generic flight-to-safety. Gold has four known structural failures: confiscation at handoff, with Executive Order 6102 as precedent; assay risk; settlement cost scaling with mass; and supply inflation at roughly 1.5\% annually.


\begin{definition}[Capturable System]
A monetary system in which at least one of P1--P7 fails for its
underlying settlement asset. Fiat, sovereign bonds, and equities
have no exit valve: equities admit no bearer instrument separable
from the share, and fiat and bonds have no underlying outside the
issuing system. Gold and Bitcoin offer redemption to a
self-custodiable bearer asset. Gold terminates in physical gold,
which retains the four failures above. Bitcoin ETFs can be
redeemed in steps to self-custodied Bitcoin, which satisfies P1--P7.
\end{definition}

Alternate consensus ledgers fail to satisfy P1--P7 (and are therefore Capturable) through any of four channels: identifiable founders or foundations violate P6; validator, treasury, or premine concentration violates P1, P3, and P6; consensus and governance complexity weakens P5 and P7; insufficient adversarial exposure leaves P7 unvalidated. P7 is structurally distinct: P1--P6 can be designed into a candidate ledger, but P7 is earned only by surviving an open adversarial attack surface.

\begin{definition}[Exit]
The action of moving capital from a capturable system to a neutral
settlement asset. The complement is \emph{Stay}: holding
capital in the prevailing capturable system.
\end{definition}

\subsection{3.3 Game}

The Exit Game is a non-cooperative game in which each capital allocator chooses one of two actions: hold capital in the prevailing capturable system (Stay) or move it to a neutral settlement asset (Exit). Exit is not free. Early exit to Bitcoin requires a social cost to act against peer consensus. Depending on jurisdiction and employment, exit to Bitcoin can also cost real legal or reputational damage.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Players.} $N$ is the population of capital allocators, weighted by their share $a_i$ of total capturable capital. $\kappa_i > 0$ is how much social cost allocator $i$ pays for moving before peers. $\rho_i > 0$ is how exposed allocator $i$ is to state penalty for the move itself. Fiduciaries answering to boards, committees, or constituencies sit at the high end of $\kappa_i$. Individuals and unilateral sovereigns sit lower. Both weights vary continuously across allocators.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Objective.} Preserve real purchasing power. Preference for capturable versus neutral assets is a pure function of the Exit Game.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Rules.} Each allocator chooses what share of capital sits in Exit versus Stay. The choice is not a one-time flip but an ongoing reallocation: sell Stay to buy Exit, or sell Exit to re-enter Stay. Switching assets incurs friction; positions are sticky. Only the net aggregate $p_t$ matters. As $p$ rises, Exit pays more and Stay pays less. \S3.4.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Dynamics.} Two things grow slowly and do not reverse: more allocators come to see that staying in the system is losing them real money, and the money itself keeps getting debased. As both build, net capital moves into Exit. \S3.5.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Payoffs.} The game is $G = (N, S, u)$ in standard non-cooperative form (Fudenberg and Tirole\textsuperscript{14}), with $S = \prod_i S_i$, $S_i = \{\text{Exit}, \text{Stay}\}$, $\sum_i a_i = 1$, and $p_t = \sum_{i \in \text{Exit}_t} a_i$.
\[
u_i^E(p) := R_B(p) - \kappa_i K_A(p) - \rho_i R_A(p),
\]
\[
u_i^S(p) := R_F - K_N(p).
\]

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item $R_B(p)$: real return on the neutral asset.
\item $R_F$: return on capturable assets.
\item $K_A(p)$: conformity cost of acting before peers (Katz and Shapiro\textsuperscript{17}; Farrell and Saloner\textsuperscript{11}).
\item $R_A(p)$: realization and regulatory friction; gains tax, fees, \textit{etc}.
\item $K_N(p)$: non-adoption penalty.
\end{itemize}

\smallskip
\noindent Weights are strictly positive and differ across allocators. $R_B$, $K_A$, $R_A$, $K_N$ are continuous and bounded.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption*{\textbf{Table 2.} Normal-form payoff matrix for player $i$, with $u_i^E$, $u_i^S$ as defined above.}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
 & Others: Stay & Others: Exit \\
\midrule
Player $i$: Stay & $u_i^S(0)$ & $u_i^S(p)$ \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{3.4 Monotonicity Conditions}

Milgrom and Shannon\textsuperscript{20} showed that when the payoff
gap between two options widens as some parameter rises, the player's
best response moves toward the favored option and does not return.
This paper applies that result with adoption $p$ as the parameter
and Exit as the favored option: as more actors exit, Exit's gap
over Stay widens. Four conditions on the payoff components secure
that widening.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(M\arabic*),nosep]
\item $R_B'(p) > 0$: Bitcoin's return rises with adoption (network effects, Assumption~4)
\item $K_A'(p) < 0$: The cost of switching to Bitcoin falls as more allocators switch
\item $R_A'(p) < 0$: Friction eases as adoption normalizes
\item $K_N'(p) > 0$: The cost of staying in capturable assets rises as competitors exit
\end{enumerate}

\smallskip
\noindent These conditions hold on average over long
horizons rather than at every instant.

\subsection{3.5 Threshold Drift}

The capturable system's failure mode is not the overt seizure,
capital control, or hyperinflation. It is the continuous
extraction of real wealth through the interaction of nominal
asset appreciation and the taxation of nominal gains. As
debasement raises the price level, equities, real estate, and
similar capturable assets appreciate nominally to preserve real
value. Holders are then taxed on the nominal gain. Post-tax real
returns are negative (Feldstein\textsuperscript{12}). Public debt keeps growing; private
claims lose real value to service the debt. Nominally, balances go up. The loss only shows in real terms. Multipolarity (Assumption~1) leaves debasement unchecked. The tax wedge on nominal gains then produces $R_F < 0$ in real terms.
Allocators accept the slow erosion because money illusion
(Fisher\textsuperscript{13}) reads nominal stability as safety
and treats lumpy real outperformance as risk.

The macro state evolves on two slowly-varying indices:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item $K_t \in [0,1]$ (\emph{recognition}): the share of capital allocators who recognize capturable assets as financially repressed. Recognition accumulates gradually rather than arriving with a discrete capture event.
\item $D_t \in [0, \infty)$ (\emph{debasement}): a debasement-intensity index summarizing three post-1945 financial-repression channels: debt-to-GDP overhang (IMF\textsuperscript{16}), the inflation--capital-gains tax wedge (Feldstein\textsuperscript{12}), and the rate-cap and reserve-requirement toolkit (Reinhart and Sbrancia\textsuperscript{23}).
\end{itemize}
Both $K_t$ and $D_t$ rise slowly and do not reverse on the
horizons relevant to Theorem~1.

Let $\Delta_i(p; K, D) := u_i^E(p) - u_i^S(p)$ denote player $i$'s Exit minus Stay payoff gap at adoption $p$ and macro state $(K, D)$. Each allocator's threshold depends on $(K, D)$:
\[
p_i^*(K, D) := \inf\{p \in [0, 1] : \Delta_i(p; K, D) > 0\}.
\]
Assume the sign conditions
\[
\frac{\partial p_i^*}{\partial K} \leq 0, \qquad
\frac{\partial p_i^*}{\partial D} \leq 0.
\]
These hold strictly for at least some capital allocators.
Higher $D_t$ lowers $R_F$ in real terms. Higher $K_t$ erodes
$\kappa_i K_A$ and $\rho_i R_A$. Both effects raise
$\Delta_i(0; K, D)$ and push $p_i^*$ down.

The critical mass invoked in Assumption~4 is the smallest adoption level at which at least one allocator's threshold has been crossed:
\[
p_c(K, D) := \inf\Bigl\{ p \in [0, 1] : \sum_{i\,:\,\Delta_i(p;\,K,D) > 0} a_i \;>\; 0 \Bigr\}.
\]
Above $p_c$, the M1--M4 feedbacks (Katz and Shapiro\textsuperscript{17}) prevent Stay coalitions from reforming (\S4.2). The endpoint is dominance in the network-effects sense, not full adoption $p = 1$. As $(K_t, D_t)$ accumulate, $p_c$ drifts downward (\S4.3): $p > p_c$ holds even when $p_t$ is flat. This is path-dependent adoption (Arthur\textsuperscript{3}).

The model's claims are structural and directional: existence
of thresholds $\{p_i^*\}$, sign of comparative statics, and
direction of drift in $p_c$ under accumulating $(K_t, D_t)$.
It does not predict specific values, tipping times, or
saturation levels.

An adoption level $p$ is an equilibrium of the Exit Game when
no allocator, alone or in any group that holds together, gains
by switching.

\section{4. Results}

\subsection{4.1 Monotone Exit Advantage}

First, each actor's payoff gap from Stay to Exit widens as more actors exit. Every actor therefore has a threshold above which Exit becomes the only rational move. Hold the slowly varying state $(K, D)$ fixed throughout this subsection. By convention, $p_i^* = +\infty$ when $\Delta_i$ stays non-positive on $[0, 1]$.

\begin{proposition}[Monotone Exit Advantage]
Under Assumptions~1--4 and maintained conditions (M1)--(M4):

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item The payoff differential $\Delta_i(p)$ is strictly increasing
in $p$ for all player types.
\item For each player $i$ there exists a threshold
$p_i^* \in [0, 1] \cup \{+\infty\}$ such that Exit is the unique
best response for $p > p_i^*$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Define the payoff differential and take its derivative in $p$:
\begin{align*}
\Delta_i(p) &= u_i(\text{Exit}) - u_i(\text{Stay}) \\
            &= [R_B(p) - R_F] - \kappa_i K_A(p) - \rho_i R_A(p) + K_N(p), \\
\frac{d\Delta_i}{dp} &= R_B'(p) - \kappa_i K_A'(p) - \rho_i R_A'(p) + K_N'(p).
\end{align*}

Under (M1)--(M4), each term is positive: $R_B'(p) > 0$ by (M1); $-\kappa_i K_A'(p) \geq 0$ by (M2); $-\rho_i R_A'(p) \geq 0$ by (M3); $K_N'(p) > 0$ by (M4).

Therefore $d\Delta_i/dp > 0$: the advantage of Exit rises strictly with $p$. Each player's threshold $p_i^* := \inf\{p \in [0, 1] : \Delta_i(p) > 0\}$ specializes the \S3.5 definition with $(K, D)$ fixed. For actors with $p_i^* \in [0, 1]$, the intermediate value theorem applied to the continuous $\Delta_i$ delivers $\Delta_i(p_i^*) = 0$, and for $p > p_i^*$ Exit strictly dominates.

\end{proof}

\subsection{4.2 Coalition Instability}

\S4.1 gave each actor a threshold. The group implication is sharper: no Stay coalition is self-enforcing once any one member has crossed. Stay holdouts can attempt to enforce group cohesion through coercion, but will fail per the preference falsification remark below. The first defector profits unilaterally; the actors left behind face a higher exit advantage than before.

\begin{proposition}[Coalition Instability]
Under Assumption~2 and Property~P3, no Stay coalition $C$
containing at least one actor with a crossed threshold
($p > p_j^*$ for some $j \in C$) is self-enforcing in the
Bernheim-Peleg-Whinston sense.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Consider a coalition $C \subseteq N$ maintaining Stay. For $C$ to be self-enforcing in the sense of Bernheim, Peleg, and Whinston\textsuperscript{4}, no member $j \in C$ can profit from unilateral defection. By Proposition~1, $u_j(\text{Exit}) > u_j(\text{Stay})$ when $p$ exceeds $p_j^*$. Property~P3 (open access) makes the institutional defection cost zero: no counterparty can refuse the trade and no clearing intermediary can foreclose settlement. Regulatory coercion enters the payoff as $R_A$ and is already baked into each actor's threshold; Proposition~2 takes that pricing as given. Under Assumption~2, the crossed-threshold member defects: for at least one $j \in C$, $u_j(\text{defect from } C) > u_j(\text{remain in } C)$. The coalition therefore fails Nash, and any coalition-proof refinement requires Nash.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Proposition~2 covers coalitions with at least one crossed-threshold member. Permanent holdouts ($p_j^* = +\infty$), admitted by Proposition~1, lie outside its scope: no private reason to defect. The aggregate share of capital held for ideological reasons rather than rational self-interest (Assumption~2) is assumed immaterial. The real-world case is regulatory coercion holding the coalition together: capital controls, fiduciary mandates, jurisdictional penalties. P1 limits what coercion can do. An asset controlled by secret information, not physical possession, can be seized only by forcing each holder to disclose it one at a time. Cost grows with the number of holders, not the size of any one holding. Preference falsification (Kuran\textsuperscript{18}) then separates what holders say from what they do. Aggregate $p_t$ tracks what they do. Once enough private exits register, public Stay breaks.
\end{remark}

\subsection{4.3 Ratchet}

Proposition~2 took the threshold profile as given. \S4.3 lets thresholds move. As $(K_t, D_t)$ accumulate (recognition, debasement), each $p_i^*$ drifts downward, and $p_c$ drifts with the $p_i^*$. Two claims follow. First, above $p_c$ no returning Stay coalition can form. Any candidate coalition with a crossed-threshold member is broken by Proposition~2. Any candidate coalition without a crossed-threshold member contains only allocators not moving to Exit. Second, $p_c$ is non-increasing in $t$.

\begin{theorem}[Ratchet]
Under Assumptions~1--4, maintained conditions (M1)--(M4), and
the \S3.5 sign conditions: (a) $\{p > p_c\}$
is locally stable against returning Stay coalitions. (b) Under
monotonic accumulation of $(K_t, D_t)$, the critical mass
$p_c(K_t, D_t)$ drifts monotonically downward in $t$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{(a) Local stability above $p_c$.} If $p > p_c(K, D)$, the crossed-threshold set
\[
H(p) := \{i : \Delta_i(p; K, D) > 0\}
\]
has positive capital weight. Any perturbation attempting to coordinate
a return to high-Stay above $p_c$ is a candidate Stay coalition
$C$ and falls into one of two cases. If $C \cap H(p)$ is
nonempty, Proposition~2 applies: the crossed-threshold member
defects under P3, and $C$ is not self-enforcing. If $C \cap H(p)$
is empty, $C$ consists entirely of uncrossed-threshold capital. These actors already prefer Stay at the current $p$ and do not move toward Exit; no candidate Stay coalition expands above $p_c$.
The system is locally stable above $p_c$, with $\{p > p_c\}$ acting as a focal coordination level in the sense of Schelling\textsuperscript{25}. The dynamic part, in which the finite-threshold Stay pool shrinks as $(K_t, D_t)$ accumulate, takes $p_t$ above $p_c$ in the long run. Drawdowns and cascade pauses along $p_t$ are admitted. A permanent stall would require two conditions to hold jointly: $p_t$ plateauing below $p_c$ indefinitely, and $p_c$ failing to fall. The second condition is ruled out by part (b).

\emph{(b) Drift of $p_c$.} Because $p_c$ is defined from the individual thresholds, the \S3.5 sign conditions transfer directly to $p_c$:
\[
\frac{\partial p_i^*}{\partial K} \leq 0, \quad \frac{\partial p_i^*}{\partial D} \leq 0, \quad \frac{\partial p_c}{\partial K} \leq 0, \quad \frac{\partial p_c}{\partial D} \leq 0.
\]
Under monotonic accumulation of $(K_t, D_t)$, $p_c(K_t, D_t)$ is non-increasing in $t$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{4.4 Bitcoin Exit Dominance}

The three preceding results all hold simultaneously in the Exit Game under the same hypotheses. Their conjunction is the title claim.

\begin{theorem}[Bitcoin Exit Dominance]
Under the joint hypotheses of Propositions~1--2 and Theorem~1,
the capital-weighted adoption process satisfies all
three of:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item the payoff advantage of Exit over Stay is strictly increasing
in $p$ for every player type (Proposition~1);
\item no Stay coalition with at least one crossed-threshold
member is self-enforcing (Proposition~2);
\item $\{p > p_c\}$ is locally stable, and $p_c$ drifts monotonically downward under accumulating $(K_t, D_t)$ (Theorem~1).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Proposition~1 gives (i). Proposition~2, with (i) supplying the
per-defector incentive, gives (ii). Theorem~1 gives (iii). Their
conjunction is the Bitcoin Exit Dominance claim.
\end{proof}

\section{5. Limitations}

While Bitcoin holds the longest continuous public survival record, this paper does not formally exclude future ledgers from escaping Definition~2.

A1--A4 are each independently load-bearing. A1 (persistent multipolarity) fails only if rival regimes completely coordinate and preference falsification is universally undermined. A2 (rational self-interest) fails only if net aggregate actors do not trend toward defection when defection goes unpunished. A3 (computational hardness) fails only if the underlying cryptography breaks and the protocol cannot replace the broken cryptography. A4 (persistence of network effects) fails only if the incumbency account of network goods is disproved.

\section{6. Conclusion}

Previous work on monetary coordination treats sovereign issuers as the only agents that matter. The Exit Game flips that: capital allocators decide what counts as settlement. The long-run outcome depends on whether an asset exists that no government issues. Once such an asset exists and satisfies P1--P7, the question is no longer whether allocators can leave the capturable system but whether continued debasement pushes adoption past $p_c$.

Central banks choose reserve assets by what those assets do, not only by politics. Since the Global Financial Crisis they have been accumulating gold. The Exit Game predicts the same pattern for any asset that does what gold does without gold's failures, at lower cost. \S3.5 predicts that continued debasement keeps moving more allocators out of fiat.

The Bitcoin Exit Dominance claim is new and depends on two conditions outside this paper's scope. First, no other consensus ledger holds all of P1--P7: alternatives must either fail one of the properties or fail to draw adoption. Second, autonomous AI agents that need a settlement asset choose Bitcoin rather than invent a new settlement asset. A companion paper in preparation addresses the first across consensus ledgers; a second extends neutral settlement to autonomous agents.
